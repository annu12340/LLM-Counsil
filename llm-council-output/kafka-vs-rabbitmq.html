Kafka vs RabbitMQ for Event Pipeline

A structured, adversarial debate among five expert personas, synthesized into a decisive recommendation.

Single-model baseline — no council

Kafka is usually the better choice for high-throughput event streaming and replay, while RabbitMQ is often simpler for task queues and request-driven messaging. At 10k events/sec either can work, so the choice depends on whether replay and retention are central requirements or just nice-to-have operations features.

Why this is risky: That answer is directionally useful but still hedges, treats replay as a vague requirement, and does not price the operational burden for a small team.

Topic

Should we use Kafka or RabbitMQ for an event pipeline handling about 10k events/sec with replay needs and a small ops team?

Context

The stated workload is about 10k events/sec, at-least-once delivery, a small operations team, and some need for replay. No hard retention window, consumer count, ordering boundary, or compliance requirement is specified.

The decision is not a generic broker popularity contest. It turns on whether durable replay is a product contract or only an operational recovery tool.

Inferred decision criteria
1. Replay contract — does the system need a durable event log consumers can independently rewind?
2. Operational load — can a small team safely run and debug the broker under incident pressure?
3. Delivery semantics — does the workload need queue-style work distribution or stream-style fan-out?
4. Migration trigger — is there a concrete point where the simpler broker becomes the wrong platform?

Assumption: Replay is useful but not yet specified as a long-retention product feature with many independent consumers.

🧱First-Principles Thinker: The fundamental distinction is queue versus log. If the business needs consumers to rewind history independently, that is a log-shaped problem and Kafka is the more natural primitive.
Verdict: Choose Kafka if replay is a real contract, not a debugging convenience.
🔬Research Scientist: The evidence gap is the replay requirement. Without a retention window, consumer fan-out count, and ordering boundary, picking Kafka is an assumption masquerading as architecture.

⚙️Systems Engineer: A small ops team should avoid buying a distributed log unless the log is the product. RabbitMQ can handle this rate with simpler day-two operations when the workload is mostly delivery and retry.
Verdict: Start with RabbitMQ unless durable replay is non-negotiable.
🚩Skeptic / Red Team: Both choices can be wrong if the team cannot name failure modes and retention needs. Kafka can become an ops tax, while RabbitMQ can become a migration trap if replay quietly becomes required.
Verdict: Neither until replay and retention are made explicit.
👤Product / User Advocate: Users do not care which broker is elegant; they care that events are processed and incidents are recoverable. The smaller team likely ships faster with RabbitMQ unless customers explicitly need historical replay.
Verdict: RabbitMQ is the better product move unless replay is user-visible.

⚖ Final Verdict

Best argument
The best argument was the First-Principles queue-versus-log distinction, tempered by the Systems Engineer's operational constraint: Kafka is right for durable replay, but RabbitMQ is the safer default when replay is not yet a product contract.
Biggest risk
The biggest risk is letting a vague replay requirement drift until RabbitMQ becomes a trap or Kafka becomes an unnecessary operational burden.
Key tradeoff
RabbitMQ buys lower operational load and faster delivery for a small team; Kafka buys retained history and independent rewind at the cost of more complex operations.
Consensus
All five personas converged on replay as the deciding variable. With replay underspecified, RabbitMQ is acceptable now only if the team documents the Kafka trigger.
Recommendation

Use RabbitMQ for the current pipeline, and write a hard migration trigger: move to Kafka if replay becomes a customer-visible or compliance-backed requirement, if consumers need independent rewind over a meaningful retention window, or if backfills become routine.
